In the 2023–2024 academic year, U.S. colleges distributed over $240 billion in financial aid, with roughly $130 billion coming from federal, state, and institutional grants that do not require repayment, according to the College Board’s Trends in College Pricing and Student Aid 2024 report. Yet nearly 40% of eligible students fail to file the Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA) each year, leaving an estimated $3.6 billion in federal Pell Grant funds unclaimed, per the National College Attainment Network (NCAN, 2023). Understanding the distinct eligibility criteria for grants, scholarships, work-study, and loans is the first step to accessing this funding. Each category has specific requirements tied to financial need, academic merit, demographic background, or field of study. This guide breaks down the application conditions for the major types of college funding programs available to U.S. undergraduates and international students studying in the United States.

## Federal Pell Grants: Need-Based Eligibility

**Federal Pell Grants** are the cornerstone of U.S. need-based aid, awarded exclusively to undergraduate students who demonstrate exceptional financial need. For the 2024–2025 award year, the maximum Pell Grant is $7,395, and eligibility is determined by the Student Aid Index (SAI) calculated from your FAFSA data.

To qualify, you must be a U.S. citizen or eligible non-citizen, have a valid Social Security number, and be enrolled at least half-time in an eligible degree or certificate program. The SAI threshold for Pell eligibility changes annually; for 2024–2025, students with an SAI between –$1,500 and $6,606 may receive a partial or full award. You cannot receive Pell Grants from more than one school at a time, and total lifetime eligibility is capped at 12 semesters (roughly six years). If you have a bachelor's degree already, you are ineligible, regardless of financial need.

### FAFSA Filing Requirements

The only way to apply is by submitting the FAFSA each academic year. The form opens on October 1 for the following fall. You must report income and asset data from two years prior (e.g., 2022 tax returns for the 2024–2025 cycle). Dependency status determines whether parental information is required. **Dependent students** must include parent data unless they meet specific exemptions (e.g., age 24, married, veteran status, or legal dependents).

## Institutional Merit Scholarships: Academic and Talent Criteria

Many colleges offer **institutional merit scholarships** that are not based on financial need but on academic achievement, test scores, or specialized talents. These awards can range from $1,000 per year to full tuition coverage. For example, the University of Alabama's Presidential Scholarship awards full in-state tuition plus a stipend to National Merit Finalists, while Boston University's Trustee Scholarship covers full tuition for students with a 4.0 GPA and top 5% class rank.

Eligibility typically requires a minimum high school GPA (often 3.5 or higher on a 4.0 scale) and standardized test scores (SAT 1400+ or ACT 31+ for top-tier awards). Some scholarships require separate applications, essays, or letters of recommendation submitted by November or December of senior year. **Automatic merit scholarships** are awarded based solely on GPA and test scores without a separate application—check each school's website for their published grid. For international students, many U.S. universities offer merit aid, but you must confirm that your visa status (typically F-1) does not disqualify you.

### Talent-Based and Portfolio Scholarships

Art, music, theater, and athletic scholarships fall under talent-based aid. Applicants usually must audition or submit a portfolio by a specific deadline (often December–February). Athletic scholarships are governed by NCAA rules: Division I and II schools can offer full or partial scholarships, but Division III schools cannot award athletic scholarships. For performing arts, a prescreening video or digital portfolio is often required before a live audition invitation.

## State-Funded Grant Programs: Residency Requirements

Each U.S. state operates its own **grant and scholarship programs** for residents attending in-state colleges. For instance, the California Cal Grant program awarded over $2.4 billion to 400,000+ students in 2023–2024, with eligibility requiring a minimum 3.0 high school GPA and a family income below $100,000 (for Cal Grant A). New York's Tuition Assistance Program (TAP) provides up to $5,665 per year for residents with a net taxable income under $80,000.

To qualify, you generally must be a state resident for at least 12 consecutive months before enrollment, attend a participating in-state institution, and maintain satisfactory academic progress (SAP)—typically a 2.0 GPA or higher. Some states require separate applications (e.g., Texas' TEXAS Grant requires the FAFSA and a state aid application). **Residency verification** is strict: living in a state solely for educational purposes does not establish residency. Check your state's higher education agency website for exact deadlines and GPA thresholds.

## Federal Work-Study: Part-Time Employment for Students

**Federal Work-Study (FWS)** is a need-based program that provides part-time jobs for undergraduate and graduate students, allowing them to earn money to pay for education expenses. For the 2023–2024 year, approximately 1.6 million students participated, earning an average of $1,800 annually, according to the U.S. Department of Education.

Eligibility is determined by financial need as calculated by the FAFSA. Your school's financial aid office will list your FWS eligibility amount in your award letter. You must be enrolled at least half-time and maintain SAP. Jobs can be on-campus (library, administrative offices, labs) or off-campus with approved nonprofit or public agencies. FWS earnings are not counted as income on the following year's FAFSA (up to the award amount), which protects future aid eligibility. **Priority consideration** goes to students with the highest need—typically those with an SAI of $0 or negative.

### Finding and Maintaining a Work-Study Job

You must actively apply for FWS positions through your school's job portal; the award does not guarantee a job. Most schools limit hours to 10–20 per week. If you do not use your FWS award in a given semester, it may be reduced or removed for the next term.

## Private and External Scholarships: Diverse Criteria

Thousands of **private scholarships** are offered by foundations, corporations, nonprofits, and community organizations. Total private scholarship funding in the U.S. is estimated at $7–10 billion annually, per Scholarships.com data. Eligibility criteria vary widely: some target specific majors (e.g., engineering, nursing), ethnic backgrounds (e.g., Hispanic Scholarship Fund), military families, or first-generation college students.

Application requirements typically include a completed form, transcript, one or more essays, and letters of recommendation. Deadlines are scattered throughout the year, but many peak between October and March for the following fall. **No essay scholarships** exist but are highly competitive due to low barriers. Always verify that a scholarship is legitimate—legitimate programs never charge application fees. For international students, search for scholarships that explicitly accept non-U.S. citizens (e.g., the AAUW International Fellowships).

For cross-border tuition payments, some international families use channels like [Flywire tuition payment](https://go.compares.cheap/flywire-edu-payments-2376?p=faq-college/articles/zh/%E5%A4%A7%E5%AD%A6%E5%90%84%E7%B1%BB%E8%B5%84%E5%8A%A9%E9%A1%B9%E7%9B%AE%E7%94%B3%E8%AF%B7%E6%9D%A1%E4%BB%B6%E4%B8%8E%E8%B5%84%E6%A0%BC%E8%AF%A6%E8%A7%A3) to settle fees from abroad efficiently.

## Loan Programs: Borrowing with Repayment Terms

**Federal student loans** are the most common form of borrowed aid, with the Direct Loan program disbursing over $95 billion in 2023–2024. Eligibility requires FAFSA filing, U.S. citizenship or eligible non-citizen status, and enrollment at least half-time. Direct Subsidized Loans are need-based (SAI determines eligibility); the government pays interest while you're in school. Direct Unsubsidized Loans are available regardless of need, but interest accrues immediately.

Annual borrowing limits depend on your year in school and dependency status: first-year dependent undergraduates can borrow up to $5,500 (subsidized + unsubsidized combined); independent students can borrow up to $9,500. Aggregate limits cap total borrowing at $31,000 for dependent undergraduates and $57,500 for independent undergraduates. **Parent PLUS Loans** require a credit check and are available to parents of dependent undergraduates regardless of need. Private loans are a last resort and require a creditworthy cosigner for most students.

### Graduate and Professional Students

Graduate students can borrow up to $20,500 annually in Direct Unsubsidized Loans and may also access Grad PLUS Loans (credit-based). Interest rates for 2024–2025 are fixed at 6.08% for Direct Subsidized/Unsubsidized undergraduate loans and 7.08% for Direct Unsubsidized graduate loans.

## FAQ

### Q1: Can I receive both a Pell Grant and a merit scholarship at the same time?

Yes, you can receive both simultaneously. Pell Grants are need-based and do not affect merit scholarship eligibility. In fact, many students combine Pell Grants, institutional merit awards, and state grants. For example, a student with an SAI of $2,000 might receive a $4,000 Pell Grant and a $10,000 merit scholarship from the same university. The total aid cannot exceed the cost of attendance, but there is no rule against stacking need-based and merit-based aid.

### Q2: What is the minimum GPA to maintain financial aid eligibility?

The minimum GPA for Satisfactory Academic Progress (SAP) is typically 2.0 on a 4.0 scale for undergraduate students. Federal regulations require schools to enforce SAP standards, which also include completing at least 67% of attempted credits and not exceeding 150% of the program length (e.g., 6 years for a 4-year degree). If you fall below a 2.0 GPA, you may lose aid eligibility for the next semester unless you appeal successfully.

### Q3: Do international students qualify for U.S. federal financial aid?

No, international students on F-1 or J-1 visas are not eligible for federal Pell Grants, Direct Loans, or Federal Work-Study. However, they can apply for institutional merit scholarships, private scholarships from U.S. organizations that accept non-citizens, and scholarships from their home country's government. Some U.S. universities offer need-based aid to international students (e.g., Harvard, MIT, Princeton), but this is rare and requires submitting the CSS Profile.
