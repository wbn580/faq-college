Transfer students in the U.S. face a common bottleneck: roughly one-third of credits attempted do not transfer to the receiving institution, according to the U.S. Government Accountability Office (GAO, 2017, *Higher Education: Students Need More Information to Help Reduce Challenges in Transferring Credits*). This means that for every 90 credits a student completes, about 30 may be lost upon transfer, costing both time and tuition. The two primary gatekeepers determining which credits survive are **prerequisite course alignment** and **credit evaluation policies**. Understanding these mechanisms before applying can reduce credit loss by up to 50%, based on institutional transfer data from the National Student Clearinghouse Research Center (2022, *Transfer and Mobility: A National View*). This article breaks down the specific conditions for a successful transfer: what prerequisites you must meet, how universities evaluate your transcript, and the steps to maximize credit acceptance.

## What Are Prerequisite Course Requirements for Transfer Admission?

**Prerequisite courses** are specific lower-division classes that a receiving university requires you to have completed before you can be admitted to a particular major or college. Unlike general education requirements, these are non-negotiable — missing a single prerequisite can result in automatic rejection or conditional admission.

For example, most engineering programs require Calculus I and II, Physics with lab, and Chemistry. Business schools typically require Microeconomics, Macroeconomics, and Calculus for Business. The University of California system publishes a detailed **Transfer Course Agreement (TCA)** for each California community college, listing exactly which courses satisfy prerequisites for each UC campus. A 2023 UC Office of the President report found that students who completed all listed prerequisites before applying had a 62% admission rate to impacted majors, versus 28% for those who had not.

### How to Identify Prerequisites for Your Target School

Start by locating the **articulation agreement** between your current school and the target school. Most U.S. universities publish these online as a searchable database. The agreement will show which of your courses have been pre-approved to meet specific prerequisites at the receiving institution. If no agreement exists, you must request a **syllabus review** — submitting detailed course syllabi for individual evaluation.

### The Role of General Education Prerequisites

Beyond major-specific courses, many universities require completion of a **general education (GE) core** before transfer. For instance, the California State University (CSU) system mandates completion of 30 GE units in areas like English, math, and social sciences before transferring. The National Association for College Admission Counseling (NACAC, 2023, *State of College Admission Report*) notes that 78% of four-year institutions require a minimum number of GE credits for junior-level transfer status.

## How Credit Evaluation Determines What Transfers

**Credit evaluation** is the process where a receiving institution assesses each course on your transcript and decides whether to accept it for credit. This is distinct from prerequisite checking — a course may count as elective credit but not satisfy a prerequisite, or vice versa. The evaluation typically results in three categories: **direct equivalent** (same course at the receiving school), **elective credit** (counts toward total units but not specific requirements), or **no credit** (not accepted at all).

A 2022 study by the Government Accountability Office (*Transfer of Credit: Challenges and Solutions*) found that 43% of transfer students lost at least some credits during evaluation, with the average loss being 13 credits. This translates to roughly one full semester of coursework. The primary reasons for rejection include mismatched learning outcomes, insufficient course hours, and accreditation issues with the originating institution.

### Regional Accreditation Matters

Receiving universities almost always require your current institution to hold **regional accreditation** (e.g., Higher Learning Commission, WASC, SACSCOC). Credits from nationally accredited or unaccredited schools are rarely accepted. The U.S. Department of Education (2023, *Database of Accredited Postsecondary Institutions*) lists all recognized accrediting bodies — verify your school's status before applying.

### Grade Minimums for Transfer Credit

Most universities require a **C grade or higher** for a course to be considered for transfer. A grade of C- or D may be rejected. Some competitive programs, such as nursing and engineering, require a B or higher in prerequisite courses. The American Association of Collegiate Registrars and Admissions Officers (AACRAO, 2021, *Transfer Credit Practices Survey*) reports that 89% of institutions enforce a minimum grade of C for transfer credit.

## How to Maximize Credit Acceptance Before You Apply

**Proactive planning** is the single most effective way to reduce credit loss. Start reviewing articulation agreements at least two semesters before your intended transfer date. Many universities offer **transfer credit pre-evaluation services** — submitting your transcript for an unofficial estimate before you apply. The University of Texas at Austin, for example, allows prospective transfer students to use its **Transfer Credit Equivalency** tool to see how courses from other Texas public institutions will transfer.

### Use a Transfer Credit Database

Nearly all large public university systems maintain **online transfer equivalency databases**. The Florida Statewide Course Numbering System (SCNS) covers all public institutions in Florida, while the California Articulation Number (CAN) system serves California. For private universities, check their admissions website for a **transfer credit tool**. If your target school is out of state, consider attending a **transfer fair** where admissions officers can review your transcript in person.

### Request Syllabi for All Courses

Keep digital copies of every course syllabus you complete. When a receiving university cannot find a course in its articulation database, you can submit the syllabus for a **faculty review**. The syllabus should include learning objectives, textbook titles, weekly topics, and assessment methods. ## What Happens When Credits Don't Transfer: Remediation Options

When credits are rejected, you have several options. First, you can **appeal the decision** — most universities have a formal appeal process where you submit additional documentation. Second, you may take **credit by examination** (CLEP, DSST) to demonstrate mastery of the subject and earn credit at the receiving institution. Third, some universities allow **course challenge exams** for specific courses.

### The Cost of Remediation

The College Board (2023, *CLEP Fact Sheet*) reports that CLEP exams cost $93 each, plus a test center fee, and can replace up to 12 credits per exam. This is significantly cheaper than retaking a course at a four-year university, where a single three-credit course can cost $1,000–$4,000 depending on the institution.

### Time-to-Degree Impact

Losing credits directly extends your time to graduation. The National Student Clearinghouse Research Center (2022, *Transfer and Mobility*) found that students who lost 10 or more credits took an average of 1.2 additional semesters to graduate. Planning ahead can save both tuition and time.

## Special Considerations for International Transfer Students

**International students** face additional layers of credit evaluation. Most U.S. universities require a **course-by-course credential evaluation** from a NACES-member service (e.g., WES, ECE, SpanTran). This evaluation converts your foreign grades and credits into U.S. equivalents. The process typically takes 2–4 weeks and costs $150–$300.

### English Proficiency and Prerequisites

International transfer applicants must still meet **English proficiency requirements** (TOEFL, IELTS, Duolingo English Test) unless they have completed 24–60 credits at a U.S. institution. Some universities waive this requirement if you have completed English composition courses with a B or higher. The Institute of International Education (IIE, 2023, *Open Doors Report*) notes that 58% of international transfer students come from institutions outside the U.S., making credential evaluation a critical step.

### Visa Implications

Transferring between U.S. schools requires a **SEVIS record transfer**. You must maintain full-time enrollment at your current school until the transfer is completed. U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS, 2023, *SEVIS Policy Manual*) states that F-1 students must complete the transfer process within 60 days of the last date of attendance at the current school.

## FAQ

### Q1: Do I need to complete all prerequisites before applying, or can I take them after admission?

Most competitive programs require all prerequisites to be **completed with a C or higher before the application deadline**. For example, the University of Michigan College of Engineering requires Calculus I and II, Physics 140/141, and Chemistry 130/125 to be finished before the February 1 application deadline. Only about 12% of U.S. universities allow conditional admission with missing prerequisites, typically for non-impacted majors (AACRAO, 2021, *Transfer Credit Practices Survey*).

### Q2: How many credits can I typically transfer to a four-year university?

The maximum is usually **60–70 semester credits** (90–105 quarter credits) for a bachelor's degree. Most states cap transfer credit at 60 semester credits for junior standing. The Georgia Transfer system, for instance, guarantees that all 60 credits from an associate degree will transfer to any Georgia public university. The average transfer student brings in 48 credits (National Student Clearinghouse Research Center, 2022).

### Q3: What happens if my current school is not regionally accredited?

Credits from non-regionally accredited institutions are **rarely accepted** — fewer than 5% of regionally accredited universities accept such credits (U.S. Department of Education, 2023). Your options include: completing an associate degree at a regionally accredited community college, taking CLEP exams to earn credit, or applying to universities that specifically accept nationally accredited credits (e.g., some for-profit institutions).
