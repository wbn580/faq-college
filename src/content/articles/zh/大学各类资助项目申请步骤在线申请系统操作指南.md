In the 2022–2023 academic year, U.S. colleges and universities distributed over $241 billion in financial aid to undergraduate and graduate students, according to the College Board’s *Trends in College Pricing and Student Aid 2023* report. Of that total, roughly $123 billion came from federal loans, $59 billion from institutional grants, and $39 billion from federal grants such as Pell Grants. For international students, the picture is narrower but significant: approximately 7% of all international undergraduates receive institutional merit-based scholarships, per the Institute of International Education’s *Open Doors 2023* report. Navigating these funding options requires submitting applications through an online system — and the steps differ by aid type. This guide breaks down the exact application procedures for federal aid (FAFSA), institutional merit scholarships, need-based aid, and external grants, with a focus on the online portals you’ll use.

## Federal Aid: FAFSA Online Application

**The Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA)** is the gateway to all U.S. federal student aid — Pell Grants, federal student loans, and work-study programs. The form is free and processed through the official FAFSA website (studentaid.gov). You must submit it annually; for the 2024–2025 award year, the application opened on December 31, 2023, a shift from the traditional October 1 start.

**Step 1: Create an FSA ID.** Both the student and a parent (if the student is a dependent) need a Federal Student Aid (FSA) ID — a username and password. This acts as your electronic signature. Create it at studentaid.gov at least 2–3 days before filing, as the system may take up to 72 hours to verify your Social Security number.

**Step 2: Gather documents.** You’ll need your Social Security number, driver’s license (if any), 2022 federal tax returns (for the 2024–2025 cycle), W-2 forms, bank statements, and records of untaxed income. For non-citizen eligible noncitizens, an Alien Registration number is required.

**Step 3: Complete the form.** Log in to the FAFSA portal, select the correct award year, and fill out the student and parent sections. The system uses the **IRS Data Retrieval Tool** to auto-import tax data — use it to reduce error and verification risk. Submit and receive a **Student Aid Report (SAR)** within 3–5 days. The SAR includes your **Student Aid Index (SAI)**, which replaced the Expected Family Contribution (EFC) in 2024.

## Institutional Merit Scholarships: University-Specific Portals

**Merit-based scholarships** are awarded by individual universities based on academic achievement, test scores, or talent — not financial need. Each institution runs its own online application system, often integrated with the admission portal.

**Step 1: Check the scholarship deadline.** Many universities set a priority deadline — often November 1 or December 1 for fall admission. Missing it means automatic disqualification. For example, the University of Southern California’s **Merit Scholarship** deadline is December 1, while the University of Michigan’s **Stamps Scholarship** requires a separate application by November 15.

**Step 2: Locate the scholarship section in the admission portal.** After submitting your Common App or Coalition App, log into the university’s applicant portal. Look for a “Scholarships” or “Financial Aid” tab. Some schools, like Boston University, automatically consider all applicants for merit aid; others require a separate essay or portfolio upload.

**Step 3: Submit supplemental materials.** Common requirements include a personal statement (500–800 words), letters of recommendation (often 2), and an activities resume. For talent-based scholarships (music, art, athletics), you may need to upload a video audition or portfolio via the portal’s media upload tool — file size limits typically cap at 25 MB per file.

## Need-Based Aid: CSS Profile vs. Institutional Forms

**Need-based aid** is determined by the student’s financial circumstances. For U.S. citizens and permanent residents, the FAFSA suffices. For international students and some private universities, the **CSS Profile** — run by the College Board — is the standard tool. Over 400 colleges and scholarship programs require it, including all Ivy League schools.

**Step 1: Determine which form(s) your school requires.** Check each university’s financial aid website. For example, Harvard requires both the FAFSA and CSS Profile for domestic students, but only the CSS Profile for international applicants. The CSS Profile costs $25 for the initial application and $16 per additional school (fee waivers available for low-income students).

**Step 2: Register and complete the CSS Profile.** Go to cssprofile.collegeboard.org. Create an account, add your schools (up to 20), and fill out the detailed financial questionnaire. Unlike the FAFSA, the CSS Profile asks about home equity, business assets, and non-custodial parent income. Expect 45–60 minutes to complete.

**Step 3: Upload supporting documents.** Some schools require tax returns, W-2 forms, and business/farm statements uploaded directly to the CSS Profile portal or the university’s own financial aid portal. The **IDOC** (Institutional Documentation Service) platform handles document collection for over 300 schools — you’ll receive an email with upload instructions after submitting the CSS Profile.

## External Scholarships: Third-Party Application Portals

**External scholarships** are funded by private organizations, foundations, or corporations — not by the university or government. Examples include the **Gates Scholarship** (full-ride for Pell-eligible minority students) and the **Coca-Cola Scholars Program** ($20,000). These use independent online application systems.

**Step 1: Create a profile on scholarship search platforms.** Use databases like Fastweb, Scholarships.com, or the College Board’s BigFuture. These aggregate thousands of opportunities and match you based on GPA, major, ethnicity, and location. For international students, platforms like **IEFA.org** (International Education Financial Aid) list scholarships that do not require U.S. citizenship.

**Step 2: Read eligibility criteria carefully.** Many external scholarships have GPA cutoffs (e.g., 3.5 or higher), residency requirements, or specific major restrictions. The **Gates Scholarship** requires a minimum 3.3 GPA and U.S. citizenship or permanent residency. International students should filter for “open to all nationalities.”

**Step 3: Submit through the scholarship’s own portal.** Each organization runs its own system — there is no central platform. For the **Coca-Cola Scholars Program**, you apply via coca-colascholars.org, upload transcripts, and submit two essays (1,000 words total). Deadlines are typically in October or November for the following fall. Track submission confirmations; some portals send a confirmation email within 24 hours, others do not.

## Work-Study and On-Campus Employment: University HR Systems

**Federal Work-Study (FWS)** is a need-based program that provides part-time jobs for U.S. citizens and eligible noncitizens. International students on F-1 visas cannot work off-campus under FWS, but they can seek on-campus employment through university job portals.

**Step 1: Check your FAFSA for work-study eligibility.** If you indicated interest in work-study on the FAFSA, your SAR will show an “FWS” indicator. Not all eligible students are awarded — funds are limited. In 2022–2023, about 1.6 million students participated in FWS, per the U.S. Department of Education.

**Step 2: Search for jobs on the university’s career portal.** Most schools use platforms like Handshake, Symplicity, or a custom system (e.g., **UChicago CareerAdvance**). Filter by “On-Campus” and “Work-Study” if applicable. International students should confirm the job is classified as “on-campus employment” (e.g., library assistant, research assistant, dining hall worker).

**Step 3: Apply and complete hiring paperwork.** Submit a resume and cover letter through the portal. Once hired, you’ll need to complete I-9 and W-4 forms (for U.S. citizens) or an I-9 plus a Social Security card application (for international students). The hiring department typically initiates the process through the university’s HR system.

## State Aid: Separate Online Applications

**State-funded grants and scholarships** — such as the **Cal Grant** (California) or **Texas Grant** — require a separate application beyond the FAFSA. Each state administers its own program through a dedicated portal.

**Step 1: File the FAFSA by your state’s deadline.** Many states use the FAFSA as the base application but set earlier deadlines. For example, California’s Cal Grant requires FAFSA submission by March 2 for priority consideration. Illinois’ **Monetary Award Program (MAP)** uses the FAFSA but has a limited fund pool — apply as soon as possible after October 1.

**Step 2: Complete any supplemental state forms.** Some states require additional forms. The **New York State Tuition Assistance Program (TAP)** requires a separate TAP application via the HESC portal (hesc.ny.gov) after submitting the FAFSA. The TAP application takes about 15 minutes and asks for New York State residency verification.

**Step 3: Check your state’s grant portal for status.** Log into the state’s financial aid portal (e.g., **WebGrants** for California, **HESC** for New York) to view award status, disbursement dates, and any required verification documents. Disbursements typically occur at the start of each semester, contingent on full-time enrollment (12 credits for undergraduates).

## FAQ

### Q1: Can I apply for federal aid if I am an international student?
No. Federal student aid (Pell Grants, Direct Loans, Work-Study) is only available to U.S. citizens, U.S. nationals, and eligible noncitizens (permanent residents, refugees, asylees). International students on F-1 or J-1 visas cannot receive federal aid. However, they can apply for institutional merit scholarships, private scholarships, and on-campus employment through university portals.

### Q2: How long does it take for the FAFSA to be processed after submission?
The FAFSA is typically processed within 3–5 business days. You will receive a **Student Aid Report (SAR)** via email. If you used the IRS Data Retrieval Tool, processing may be faster. During peak periods (January–March), delays of up to 7–10 days are possible. Check your SAR for errors — corrections can be submitted online within 45 days of the original submission.

### Q3: Can I submit the CSS Profile after the FAFSA deadline?
Yes, but it may affect your aid eligibility. The CSS Profile has its own deadlines, often earlier than the FAFSA. For example, many private universities set a CSS Profile priority deadline of November 15 for Early Decision applicants. Late submissions reduce your chances of receiving need-based institutional grants, as funds are distributed on a first-come, first-served basis. Always check each school’s financial aid website for exact dates.
