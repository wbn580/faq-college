University rankings drive international student decisions. But the four major systems rank universities on completely different criteria — a university ranked 30th by QS may be 100th by ARWU. Understanding what each ranking measures helps you avoid over-relying on a single number.

## QS World University Rankings

**Weighting**: Academic Reputation 40%, Employer Reputation 10%, Faculty/Student Ratio 20%, Citations per Faculty 20%, International Faculty 5%, International Students 5%.

**What it measures**: Academic prestige (via survey of ~130,000 academics). Biased toward older, well-known comprehensive universities and universities in English-speaking countries (survey language is English).

**Best for**: Students who plan to work internationally (employer reputation component).

## THE World University Rankings

**Weighting**: Teaching 29.5%, Research Environment 29%, Research Quality 30%, International Outlook 7.5%, Industry 4%.

**What it measures**: Balanced across teaching and research. More methodologically rigorous than QS (less reliance on reputation surveys) but still favours large comprehensive universities.

**Best for**: Research students assessing research environment.

## ARWU (Shanghai Ranking)

**Weighting**: Alumni Nobel/Fields 10%, Staff Nobel/Fields 20%, Highly Cited Researchers 20%, Nature & Science publications 20%, Papers indexed 20%, Per-capita performance 10%.

**What it measures**: Pure research output. Disregards teaching quality, student experience, and employability entirely. Favours large universities with medical schools (Nobel prizes, Nature/Science papers).

**Best for**: Hard sciences and medicine research assessment.

## US News Best Global Universities

Similar to ARWU: heavily weighted toward research output (publications, citations, international collaboration). Includes a regional reputation survey component that QS and THE lack.

## Using Rankings Sensibly

1. **Use rankings as a band, not a position**: The difference between #30 and #40 is noise. Group universities into bands (top 50, 50–100, 100–200, etc.).

2. **Subject rankings are more meaningful than overall rankings**: A university ranked #200 overall may be #15 in a specific discipline.

3. **Rankings don't measure what matters to undergraduates**: Teaching quality, student satisfaction, employability at the program level, cost of living, and post-study work rights are all invisible in global rankings.

4. **Watch for "ranking management"**: Universities actively manage their position on rankings — recruiting international faculty, optimising citation practices, investing in reputation surveys. Rankings partly reflect strategic effort, not just academic quality.

Bottom line: Rankings are a useful starting filter but a poor sole decision criterion. Combine with program-level research, cost analysis, and post-study pathway assessment before choosing.
