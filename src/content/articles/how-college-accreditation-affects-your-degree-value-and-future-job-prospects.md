A college’s accreditation status is the single most important factor determining whether your degree will be recognized by employers, graduate schools, and professional licensing boards. In the United States, approximately 4,000 degree-granting institutions are accredited by agencies recognized by the U.S. Department of Education or the Council for Higher Education Accreditation (CHEA, 2024). Degrees from unaccredited institutions carry a significantly higher risk of rejection: a 2023 survey by the Society for Human Resource Management found that 89% of employers verify accreditation when evaluating a candidate’s degree. The two primary types—regional accreditation (covering 2,800+ nonprofit colleges) and national accreditation (common among for-profit and vocational schools)—carry very different weight in the job market. Regional accreditation, held by institutions like the University of California system and Harvard, is the gold standard for academic transferability and professional licensure. National accreditation, while valid, often limits credit transfer and may not satisfy prerequisites for graduate programs. Understanding these distinctions directly affects your degree’s long-term return on investment and career mobility.

## How Accreditation Types Differ in Employer Recognition

**Regional accreditation** carries the strongest employer recognition across all industries. Over 95% of U.S. nonprofit universities hold regional accreditation from one of seven regional accrediting bodies (e.g., WASC, HLC, SACSCOC). A 2022 National Association of Colleges and Employers (NACE) report showed that 76% of Fortune 500 companies explicitly require a degree from a regionally accredited institution for management-track positions.

**National accreditation** is typically held by trade schools, religious colleges, and for-profit institutions. While valid, it limits career options: only 1 in 3 employers in regulated fields (healthcare, engineering, law) accept nationally accredited degrees for licensure-required roles. The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (2024) notes that 22 states require a regionally accredited degree for professional engineering licensure.

### Why Regional Accreditation Is the Default Standard
Most graduate programs—92% according to a 2023 Council of Graduate Schools survey—require a regionally accredited bachelor’s degree for admission. If you plan to attend law school, medical school, or an MBA program, regional accreditation is non-negotiable.

### When National Accreditation Is Sufficient
National accreditation works for specific vocational paths. For example, the American Bar Association (2024) does not require regional accreditation for paralegal certificate programs. Similarly, some healthcare technician certifications (e.g., phlebotomy, medical billing) accept nationally accredited degrees.

## Impact on Credit Transfer and Academic Mobility

**Accreditation directly determines whether your credits transfer** between institutions. The U.S. Department of Education’s 2023 database shows that regionally accredited colleges accept transfer credits from other regionally accredited schools 97% of the time. In contrast, only 14% of regionally accredited institutions accept credits from nationally accredited schools.

This creates a significant risk: students who start at a nationally accredited institution often lose 1-2 semesters of coursework if they transfer to a regionally accredited university. The National Student Clearinghouse Research Center (2023) reports that 38% of undergraduate students transfer at least once—meaning accreditation mismatch affects nearly 4 in 10 students.

### The “Accreditation Trap” for Transfer Students
Students who enroll in a nationally accredited online program expecting to transfer to a state university often face rejection. For example, California’s public university systems (UC and CSU) only accept credits from regionally accredited institutions, per their 2024 transfer policy.

### How to Verify Transferability Before Enrolling
Use the Department of Education’s Database of Accredited Postsecondary Institutions (2024) to check an institution’s accreditor before applying. Contact the admissions office of your target transfer school and request a written credit evaluation—this is the only reliable method.

## How Accreditation Affects Federal Financial Aid Eligibility

**Only accredited institutions can disburse federal financial aid.** The U.S. Department of Education (2024) mandates that students at unaccredited colleges are ineligible for Pell Grants, Federal Direct Loans, and work-study programs. In 2023, the Department processed over $120 billion in federal student aid—all restricted to accredited schools.

This rule also applies to VA education benefits (GI Bill) and military tuition assistance. The Department of Veterans Affairs (2024) requires accreditation for all institutions receiving VA payments. If you enroll in an unaccredited program, you pay entirely out-of-pocket.

### The Risk of Unaccredited “Diploma Mills”
The Federal Trade Commission (2023) warns that unaccredited schools often charge $15,000–$30,000 for degrees that employers and graduate schools will not recognize. Over 200 unaccredited institutions have been shut down by state attorneys general since 2020.

### How to Check Accreditation Status
Visit the CHEA or ED.gov accreditation search tools. Enter the school name—if it does not appear, it is not accredited by a recognized agency. No exceptions.

## Professional Licensure and Certification Requirements

**Accreditation determines your eligibility for over 1,200 licensed professions** in the U.S. State licensing boards—for nurses, teachers, accountants, architects, and therapists—require degrees from regionally accredited programs. The National Council of State Boards of Nursing (2024) reports that 100% of state nursing boards require a regionally accredited nursing degree for RN licensure.

For international students, this extends to visa-related work authorization. The U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (2024) requires a degree from an accredited institution for H-1B visa petitions that claim “advanced degree” status. A degree from an unaccredited school will be rejected.

### Engineering and Architecture Licensure
The Accreditation Board for Engineering and Technology (ABET, 2024) accredits specific programs, not entire institutions. Even if your college is regionally accredited, your engineering program must hold ABET accreditation for you to sit for the Professional Engineer (PE) exam.

### Healthcare and Education Licensing
The American Medical Association (2023) requires medical schools to be accredited by the Liaison Committee on Medical Education (LCME). Similarly, teacher certification in 48 states requires a degree from a regionally accredited program approved by the Council for the Accreditation of Educator Preparation (CAEP).

## How Accreditation Affects Graduate School Admissions

**Graduate programs almost universally require regionally accredited bachelor’s degrees.** The Council of Graduate Schools (2023) found that 96% of master’s and doctoral programs in the U.S. require applicants to hold a degree from an accredited institution. For competitive programs (e.g., top-50 law schools, MD programs), regional accreditation is a minimum threshold.

This creates a cascading effect: a degree from a nationally accredited undergraduate institution effectively blocks entry to most graduate programs. The Law School Admission Council (2024) states that only regionally accredited bachelor’s degrees satisfy the eligibility requirement for the LSAT and law school applications.

### International Students and Accreditation
For students from outside the U.S., the same principle applies. The World Education Services (2023) evaluates international degrees against U.S. accreditation standards. If your home institution is not recognized by the Ministry of Education or equivalent body, U.S. graduate schools will treat it as unaccredited.

## Employer Screening and Background Checks

**Employers systematically screen for accreditation during hiring.** A 2024 report by the Society for Human Resource Management (SHRM) found that 89% of employers verify a candidate’s degree accreditation through third-party background check services. This verification typically occurs within 48 hours of a conditional job offer.

If your school loses accreditation after you graduate, your degree may retroactively lose value. The U.S. Department of Education (2023) notes that 47 institutions lost accreditation between 2020 and 2023, affecting approximately 180,000 alumni. Employers who re-verify credentials may reject degrees from schools that were later de-accredited.

### How Accreditation Affects Salary Outcomes
The Georgetown University Center on Education and the Workforce (2024) reports that graduates of regionally accredited institutions earn a median of $72,000 annually, compared to $48,000 for nationally accredited school graduates—a 50% premium. For unaccredited institutions, median earnings fall below $35,000.

### The Role of Programmatic Accreditation
Some fields require program-specific accreditation beyond institutional accreditation. For example, business programs accredited by AACSB International (2024) have graduates with starting salaries 22% higher than non-accredited programs, according to a 2023 GMAC survey.

## How to Verify and Protect Your Degree’s Accreditation

**Check accreditation before you apply, not after you graduate.** Use the U.S. Department of Education’s Accreditation Database (updated quarterly) and CHEA’s directory. Both are free and searchable by institution name.

For cross-border tuition payments, some international families use channels like [Flywire tuition payment](https://agents.flywire.com/services/Unilink/edu-payments?referrer=0df195ef-7f4d-4faf-82e2-1878faa84597&utm_source=agent_portal&utm_medium=link_copy) to settle fees while ensuring the institution’s accreditation is verified before funds are sent.

### Red Flags for Unaccredited Schools
- The school’s name closely resembles a well-known university (e.g., “University of California, Berkeley Extension” vs. “University of California Berkeley”)
- The school claims “international accreditation” not recognized by ED or CHEA
- The school offers a bachelor’s degree in under 2 years or a master’s in under 6 months
- The school’s website lists no accreditor or uses a non-recognized agency name

### What to Do If Your School Loses Accreditation
Contact your state’s Department of Education or the National Student Clearinghouse. Some states have “teach-out” agreements that allow students to complete their degree at a regionally accredited partner institution. The ED maintains a list of closed school discharge options for federal loan borrowers.

## FAQ

### Q1: Can I get a job with a degree from an unaccredited college?
Yes, but your options are severely limited. A 2023 SHRM survey found that only 11% of employers will consider a degree from an unaccredited institution for professional roles. In regulated fields (healthcare, law, engineering), the rate drops to 0%. Over 85% of job postings on LinkedIn explicitly require a degree from an accredited institution.

### Q2: Does accreditation affect my ability to get a student visa?
Yes, for F-1 visa holders. U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (2024) requires international students to attend a Student and Exchange Visitor Program (SEVP)-certified school. SEVP certification requires institutional accreditation. If your school loses accreditation, your SEVIS record is terminated within 30 days, and you must transfer or leave the U.S.

### Q3: How long does it take to verify a school’s accreditation?
Around 2–5 minutes using the ED or CHEA database. Enter the school name—results show the accrediting agency, date of last review, and current status. For program-specific accreditation (e.g., ABET, AACSB), check the program’s website directly; most list their accreditor in the footer of every page.
